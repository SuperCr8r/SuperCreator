      <button class="accordion-toggle level-2" type="button" aria-expanded="false">
        <span>Executive Summary</span>
        <span class="accordion-icon">+</span>
      </button>
      <div class="accordion-content" hidden>
        <p><strong>Atomic Habits</strong> is not really a book about habits. It is a book about how change actually happens in real life. James Clear argues that most people fail not because they lack ambition, discipline, or intelligence, but because they rely too heavily on goals and too little on systems. His core message is deceptively simple: if you improve your routines in tiny, repeatable ways, the results eventually compound into dramatic transformation.</p>

        <p>The book’s power lies in how practical it is. Clear takes research from psychology, neuroscience, and behavioral economics and translates it into usable operating rules. Rather than telling people to “want success more,” he shows how to design behavior so that success becomes more likely by default. This is why the book resonates with professionals, founders, athletes, and everyday readers alike.</p>

        <p>Its most important contribution is the shift from outcome-based thinking to identity-based behavior. Instead of asking, “What do I want to achieve?”, Clear pushes readers to ask, “Who do I want to become?” That subtle shift is powerful because identity is more durable than motivation. If someone sees themselves as a reader, runner, builder, or leader, their habits start reinforcing that identity.</p>

        <p>For leaders and operators, the book is especially useful because it scales beyond personal life. The same logic applies to teams, organizations, and culture. Small repeated behaviors—meeting hygiene, communication rituals, review cadence, feedback loops, documentation discipline—compound into either operational excellence or chronic dysfunction.</p>

        <p>The book succeeds because it is highly quotable, highly actionable, and immediately implementable. It gives readers a framework they can apply on the same day they read it.</p>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="accordion-subsection">
      <button class="accordion-toggle level-2" type="button" aria-expanded="false">
        <span>Background of the Book</span>
        <span class="accordion-icon">+</span>
      </button>
      <div class="accordion-content" hidden>
        <p>James Clear wrote <strong>Atomic Habits</strong> after years of writing about decision-making, behavior change, and continuous improvement on his website and newsletter. The book emerged from his attempt to answer a practical question: Why do smart, motivated people still fail to change consistently? His answer is that behavior is usually a design problem, not a character flaw.</p>

        <p>The title itself is meaningful. “Atomic” refers both to tiny and fundamental—small habits that serve as the basic units of larger transformation. The book was written to help people build good habits, break bad ones, and make change repeatable rather than motivationally dependent.</p>
      </div>
    </div>

His credibility comes less from academic positioning and more from his ability to synthesize research into simple, usable frameworks for everyday life and work.</p>

It explains why willpower is overrated, why environment matters more than people think, and why identity is the hidden engine of sustained change.     <div class="accordion-subsection">
      <button class="accordion-toggle level-2" type="button" aria-expanded="false">
        <span>Critical View / Devil’s Advocate</span>
        <span class="accordion-icon">+</span>
      </button>
      <div class="accordion-content" hidden>
        <p>This is a strong book, but it is not flawless.</p>

        <p>Its biggest limitation is that it can sometimes over-index on individual agency. Many habits are not merely design issues; they are also shaped by structural realities such as burnout, trauma, poverty, chronic stress, family load, or mental health conditions. The book is strongest for people who already have some degree of agency and room to optimize.</p>

        <p>It is also somewhat repetitive. The framework is powerful, but many chapters reinforce the same underlying ideas through different stories. That helps retention, but some readers may feel the core message could have been delivered in fewer pages.</p>

        <p>Finally, the book is stronger on habit initiation than on navigating truly messy human complexity—identity conflict, emotional avoidance, grief, or deep behavioral resistance.</p>
      </div>
    </div>

      <div class="accordion-content" hidden>
        <p>For leaders, the biggest lesson is this: <strong>culture is just collective habit.</strong></p>

        <p>Organizations rarely fail because of one dramatic strategic mistake. They fail because small repeated behaviors create drag:</p>
        <ul>
          <li>unclear ownership,</li>
          <li>sloppy follow-up,</li>
          <li>meeting overload,</li>
          <li>weak review loops,</li>
          <li>tolerance for mediocrity.</li>
        </ul>

        <p>The book’s framework can be directly applied to leadership systems:</p>
        <ul>
          <li>Make good behaviors obvious → dashboards, rituals, visible expectations</li>
          <li>Make them attractive → recognition, social proof, incentives</li>
          <li>Make them easy → remove bureaucracy and ambiguity</li>
          <li>Make them satisfying → close loops, celebrate wins, show progress</li>
        </ul>

        <p>A strong leader should read this book not just for self-improvement, but to ask:</p>
        <p><strong>What habits are we unintentionally teaching our teams every day?</strong></p>

  const toggles = document.querySelectorAll(".accordion-toggle");

  toggles.forEach(toggle => {
    toggle.addEventListener("click", function () {
      const container = this.closest(".accordion-section, .accordion-subsection");
      const content = this.nextElementSibling;
      const isOpen = container.classList.contains("open");

      container.classList.toggle("open", !isOpen);
      this.setAttribute("aria-expanded", String(!isOpen));

      if (content) {
        content.hidden = isOpen;
      }
    });
  });

  // Initialize default state safely
  document.querySelectorAll(".accordion-section, .accordion-subsection").forEach(section => {
    const content = section.querySelector(":scope > .accordion-content");
    const toggle = section.querySelector(":scope > .accordion-toggle");
    const isOpen = section.classList.contains("open");

    if (content) content.hidden = !isOpen;
    if (toggle) toggle.setAttribute("aria-expanded", String(isOpen));
  });
});
</script>
