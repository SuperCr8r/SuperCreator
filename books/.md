<!-- ========================= SUMMARY ========================= -->
<section id="summary" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>High-Level Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Thinking, Fast and Slow explains how our minds operate using two distinct modes: System 1 (fast, automatic, emotional) and System 2 (slow, effortful, logical). Kahneman catalogs cognitive biases, heuristics, and systematic errors that arise from reliance on System 1, and describes how System 2 can be engaged — imperfectly — to correct mistakes. He explores topics such as prospect theory, loss aversion, anchoring, availability, substitution, and how framing influences decisions. The book blends psychological experiments, practical implications, and reflections on human rationality, with applications to economics, business, medicine, and public policy.</p>

<p>Key takeaways: know when intuition is reliable, be skeptical of first impressions, anticipate common biases in judgment, and design choice environments (and personal checklists) that reduce error.</p>
</div>
</div>

</div>
</section>

<!-- ========================= CHAPTER-WISE SUMMARY ========================= -->
<section id="chapters" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Chapter-wise Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Part 1 — Two Systems</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Introduces System 1 and System 2. System 1 operates automatically and quickly with little effort; System 2 allocates attention and is lazy by default. Many errors arise because System 2 endorses System 1's suggestions without scrutiny.</p>
<ul>
<li>System 1: pattern recognition, heuristics, fast associations.</li>
<li>System 2: monitoring, reasoning, overriding impulses.</li>
<li>Implication: design tasks and environments that make errors visible to System 2 when needed.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Part 2 — Heuristics and Biases</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Examines common heuristics (anchoring, availability, representativeness) and their biases. Heuristics simplify judgment but produce systematic deviations from normative reasoning.</p>
<ul>
<li>Anchoring: initial values bias estimates even when irrelevant.</li>
<li>Availability: ease of recall skews perceived frequency and risk.</li>
<li>Representativeness: ignoring base rates and sampling variability leads to mistaken conclusions.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Part 3 — Overconfidence</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Kahneman explores illusions of understanding and skill, the planning fallacy, and the tendency to construct coherent stories from incomplete information. Experts can be unreliable when environments are noisy and feedback is weak.</p>
<ul>
<li>Hindsight bias and narrative fallacies inflate perceived predictability.</li>
<li>The planning fallacy causes systematic underestimation of time and cost.</li>
<li>Use reference classes and outside view thinking to mitigate overconfidence.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Part 4 — Choices</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Introduces prospect theory: people evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point, display loss aversion, and value probabilities nonlinearly. Preferences are constructed in context and affected by framing.</p>
<ul>
<li>Losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good (loss aversion).</li>
<li>People overweight small probabilities and underweight moderate to high probabilities.</li>
<li>Framing and default options significantly influence decisions.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Part 5 — Two Selves</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Distinguishes the experiencing self (moment-to-moment) from the remembering self (constructs retrospective evaluations). Decisions often favor the remembering self, leading to choices that maximize memorable moments rather than total experience.</p>
<ul>
<li>Duration neglect: length of an experience matters less than its peak and end.</li>
<li>Memory-based assessments drive many decisions about future experiences.</li>
<li>Design experiences with endings and peaks in mind if you want better remembered outcomes.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

</div>

<!-- ========================= EXERCISES ========================= -->
<section id="exercises" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Exercises</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Practical Exercises</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Apply the book's lessons with these exercises to build better judgment habits.</p>
<ul>
<li>Keep a decision journal for two weeks: record significant decisions, your intuitive reasons, predicted outcomes, and revisit actual outcomes after defined intervals.</li>
<li>Practice the outside view: for each project estimate, write down a reference class and use average outcomes from similar projects to adjust your plan.</li>
<li>Create an "anti-anchoring" checklist: when you receive an anchor (price, estimate), deliberately generate three alternative anchors before settling on an answer.</li>
<li>Design a choice architecture experiment: change defaults or framing for a recurring choice (e.g., saving rate, meeting scheduling) and observe behavior over a month.</li>
<li>Run a forecasting calibration exercise: make probability forecasts (e.g., will X happen by date Y?), track calibration and Brier score, and focus on improving over time.</li>
</ul>
