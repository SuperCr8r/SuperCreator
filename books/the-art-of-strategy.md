<!-- ========================= SUMMARY ========================= -->
<section id="summary" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Overview</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>The Art of Strategy teaches how to think like a strategist by applying game-theoretic reasoning to everyday and business problems. It emphasizes practical tools—sequential thinking, backward induction, Nash equilibrium, mixed strategies, commitment, and signaling—and uses clear examples and puzzles to make abstract concepts concrete.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Key Concepts</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<ul>
<li>Backward induction and subgame perfection for sequential-move games.</li>
<li>Nash equilibrium as a prediction for simultaneous-move interactions.</li>
<li>Mixed strategies to model unpredictability and avoid exploitation.</li>
<li>Commitment devices and credible threats/promises to change opponents' incentives.</li>
<li>Signaling and screening to manage asymmetric information.</li>
<li>Institutional design (auctions, bargaining rules) profoundly shapes outcomes.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

</div>
</section>

<!-- ========================= CHAPTER-WISE SUMMARY ========================= -->
<section id="chapters" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Chapter-wise Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 1 — Thinking Strategically</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Introduces strategic thinking: identify players, actions, preferences, and information. Distinguishes between cooperative and noncooperative settings and stresses modeling the situation before solving it.</p>
<ul>
<li>Practice: map strategic interactions in everyday decisions (pricing, negotiations).</li>
<li>Tip: always specify the order of moves and information available.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 2 — Sequential Games and Backward Induction</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Explains how to solve sequential-move games using backward induction to find subgame-perfect equilibria. Uses examples like entry deterrence and ultimatum bargaining.</p>
<ul>
<li>Key idea: anticipate future responses to current moves.</li>
<li>Application: credible deterrence requires that threats be optimal to carry out.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 3 — Simultaneous-Move Games and Nash Equilibrium</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Defines Nash equilibrium, best responses, and dominated strategies. Covers pure-strategy and mixed-strategy equilibria with examples like Prisoner’s Dilemma and Cournot competition.</p>
<ul>
<li>Check for dominance first, then solve best-response correspondences.</li>
<li>Mixed strategies explain unpredictability in competitive settings.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 4 — Mixed Strategies and Strategic Randomization</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Shows when and why players randomize—prevents opponents from exploiting deterministic behavior. Derives mixed equilibria from indifference conditions.</p>
<ul>
<li>Use mixing to keep opponents indifferent among their options.</li>
<li>Examples: penalty kicks, price matching when no pure equilibrium exists.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 5 — Games of Imperfect Information and Signaling</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Introduces asymmetric information: signaling (informed party sends signal) and screening (uninformed party designs mechanism). Analyzes separating and pooling equilibria.</p>
<ul>
<li>Signaling works when costly signals align incentives (education as a signal).</li>
<li>Design contracts or menus to induce self-selection.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 6 — Commitment and Credibility</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Focuses on commitment devices (binding actions that change future incentives). Distinguishes credible from incredible threats and explains strategic uses of delegation and reputation.</p>
<ul>
<li>Commitments must be verifiable and costly to reverse.</li>
<li>Reputation-building is a form of dynamic commitment in repeated settings.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 7 — Auctions and Bidding</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Examines auction formats (first-price, second-price, English, Dutch) and bidding strategies under private and common value settings. Highlights revenue equivalence conditions and winner's curse.</p>
<ul>
<li>Second-price auctions encourage truthful bidding in private-value settings.</li>
<li>Account for common-value uncertainty to avoid the winner's curse.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 8 — Bargaining and Negotiation</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Analyzes alternating-offer bargaining, Rubinstein model, and how time preferences, outside options, and commitment affect splits. Practical advice for bargaining positions and concessions.</p>
<ul>
<li>Patience and credible threats improve bargaining power.</li>
<li>Structure offers to exploit opponents' time costs.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 9 — Voting, Coalitions, and Political Games</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Applies game theory to collective decision making: plurality vs. majority rules, agenda setting, and coalition formation. Shows how institutional rules shape equilibrium outcomes.</p>
<ul>
<li>Agenda control is powerful: set the sequence to favor preferred outcomes.</li>
<li>Coalition stability depends on payoff division and external enforcement.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 10 — Applying Strategy: Case Studies</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Pulls together tools through case studies in business, politics, and everyday life—entry deterrence, pricing wars, contract design, and signaling in labor markets.</p>
<ul>
<li>Model the situation, solve for equilibria, then test robustness to information changes.</li>
<li>Simple models often yield the right intuition for complex strategic problems.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

</div>
</section>

<!-- ========================= EXERCISES ========================= -->
<section id="exercises" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Exercises</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Exercise 1 — Entry Deterrence</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Model a market with an incumbent and potential entrant. Show conditions under which the incumbent can deter entry via capacity expansion. Hint: use sequential game and backward induction to check for credible deterrence.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Exercise 2 — Mixed Strategy in Matching Pennies</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Find the mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium for matching pennies. Hint: make each player indifferent between her pure strategies.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Exercise 3 — Signaling and Education</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Construct a separating equilibrium where high-ability workers obtain costly education while low-ability workers do not. Hint: ensure costs and wage differences make the separation incentive-compatible.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Exercise 4 — Auction Strategy</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Compare bidding strategies in first-price vs. second-price sealed-bid auctions under independent private values. Which auction yields higher expected revenue? Hint: consider bid shading in the first-price case.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Exercise 5 — Ultimatum Game</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Analyze the subgame-perfect equilibrium of the ultimatum game when offers are monetary shares. How do changes in outside options affect the split? Hint: use backward induction; responder accepts any positive offer if monetary payoffs are the only consideration.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Exercise 6 — Voting and Agenda Control</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Given three alternatives and three voters with differing preferences, show how agenda setting can determine the winning alternative. Hint: construct sequential proposals and exploit pairwise majority outcomes.</p>
</div>
</div>
