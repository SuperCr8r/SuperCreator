<!-- ========================= SUMMARY ========================= -->
<section id="summary" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Overview</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>The Innovator's Dilemma explains why successful incumbent companies often fail when confronted with disruptive technologies. Christensen distinguishes sustaining technologies (which improve performance for existing customers) from disruptive technologies (which initially offer lower performance on mainstream metrics but introduce other attributes or lower cost that create new markets). Incumbents prioritize current customers and margins, which leads them to ignore nascent disruptions. To respond, organizations need separate structures — different resource allocation processes, metrics, and business models — to nurture disruptive innovations until they can compete in mainstream markets.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Key Concepts</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<ul>
<li>Disruptive vs sustaining technologies</li>
<li>Value networks and why they shape firm behavior</li>
<li>Resource dependence and customer-driven decision making</li>
<li>Importance of separate organizational structures for disruption</li>
<li>Small markets can be unattractive to incumbents but essential for disruptors</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

</div>
</section>

<!-- ========================= CHAPTER-WISE SUMMARY ========================= -->
<section id="chapter-summary" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Chapter-wise Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Introduction</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Introduces the central dilemma: why good management practices can lead to failure when industries shift due to disruptive technologies. Sets up the distinction between sustaining and disruptive innovations and outlines the book's investigative approach using industry case studies.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 1 — How Can Great Firms Fail?</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Examines historical cases where leading firms lost dominance. Identifies that adhering to profitable, existing markets and technologies makes firms blind to lower-margin, emerging technologies that later reshape industries.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 2 — Value Networks and the Impetus to Innovate</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Introduces the concept of value networks — the context in which a firm establishes its cost structure, processes, and customer relationships. Shows how these networks influence which innovations are pursued and which are ignored.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 3 — Disruptive Technological Change in Established Industries</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Details how disruptive technologies typically underperform on established metrics but offer new attributes (e.g., lower cost, simplicity) that appeal to different or emerging customers. Explains why incumbents dismiss them as unprofitable or niche.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 4 — What Goes Up, Can't Always Come Down</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Discusses the trajectory of sustaining improvements and how they overshoot the needs of some customers. This oversupply opens opportunities for simpler, cheaper disruptive options that meet underserved segments.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 5 — Managing the Strategy of Disruptive Technology</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Argues that firms should create independent organizations to explore disruptive technologies. These units need autonomy in metrics, cost structures, and distribution so they can pursue markets that are unattractive to the core business.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 6 — Deciding How to Compete</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Provides guidance for deciding whether to invest in disruptive opportunities: measure the size and growth of emerging markets, understand potential profit models, and evaluate whether a separate business model is required to pursue the opportunity.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 7 — Organizational Context and Resource Allocation</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Explains how organizational processes, incentives, and resource allocation routines bias firms toward sustaining innovations. Shows how successful resource allocation for core businesses can hinder investment into uncertain disruptive ventures.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 8 — The Role of Leadership</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Discusses leadership's role in recognizing disruptive threats and creating structures that allow for experimentation. Leadership must accept lower short-term returns in the exploratory units to capture long-term disruptive opportunities.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Conclusion</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Summarizes actionable lessons: monitor emerging technologies and markets, avoid relying solely on existing customers for resource allocation, and build separate organizations with appropriate metrics to develop disruptive innovations.</p>
</div>
</div>

</div>
</section>

<!-- ========================= EXERCISES ========================= -->
<section id="exercises" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Exercises</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Reflection Exercises</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<ul>
<li>Map your organization's value network: what customers, cost structures, and channels currently define success?</li>
<li>Identify two technologies that look “inferior” today but could enable new markets — what metrics would make them attractive?</li>
<li>List processes that currently prevent investment in low-margin or emerging markets. How could you create an autonomous team to bypass them?</li>
<li>Create a one-page business model for a disruptive spin-out aimed at an initial small market: target customer, revenue model, cost structure, and success metrics.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Practical Activities</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<ul>
<li>Interview three customers at the low end of your market and three non-consumers. What unmet needs emerge?</li>
<li>Run a six-month pilot with a dedicated budget and separate KPIs for an experiment based on a disruptive technology.</li>
<li>Conduct a post-mortem on a past product decision: were disruptive signals missed? Which organizational rules led to that outcome?</li>
</ul>
</div>
