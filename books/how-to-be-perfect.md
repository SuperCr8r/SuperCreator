<!-- ========================= Summary ========================= -->
<section id="summary" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Overview</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>How to Be Perfect blends philosophy, comedy, and practical advice to explore how we should act in morally challenging situations. The author presents clear frameworks, memorable examples, and rules of thumb to help readers make defensible moral choices without getting paralyzed by theoretical perfectionism.</p>
</div>
</div>

</div>
</section>

<!-- ========================= Chapter-wise Summary ========================= -->
<section id="chapters" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Chapter-wise Summary</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Introduction</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Sets the tone: accessible, humorous, and earnest. Introduces the problem of moral uncertainty and the goal of finding workable approaches to ethical decisions in daily life.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 1: What Is a Moral Life?</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Defines core concepts—duty, consequence, virtue—and argues for a blended approach that recognizes multiple moral lenses are useful rather than insisting on a single theory.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 2: Rules and Heuristics</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Explores rules of thumb for action (e.g., "do no unnecessary harm", "help when it's easy") and when to rely on them versus deeper deliberation.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 3: Consequences Matter</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Considers outcome-based thinking: how to weigh consequences, predict impacts, and avoid common pitfalls like short-termism or decision paralysis.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 4: Rights and Respect</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Covers rights-based considerations—autonomy, consent, and respect—and how they constrain or justify certain actions even when outcomes might suggest otherwise.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 5: The Role of Intentions</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Discusses moral psychology: how intentions shape moral judgment, the limits of blaming intentions alone, and how to balance intent with impact.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 6: Everyday Dilemmas</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Applies frameworks to common scenarios—white lies, back-seat parenting, office politics—offering actionable advice and thought experiments for practice.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 7: Moral Compromise and Negotiation</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Looks at real-world negotiation of values: when compromise is acceptable, how to prioritize competing goods, and how to preserve integrity while cooperating.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 8: Special Duties</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Explores obligations arising from relationships and roles (parent, friend, citizen) and how context changes what counts as the right action.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 9: Moral Growth</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Encourages reflection, humility, and incremental improvement: ethics as practice rather than fixed achievement, with suggestions for learning from mistakes.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Chapter 10: Putting It Together</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Combines insights into a flexible decision process: identify values, consider consequences and rights, apply heuristics, and act with charity toward oneself and others.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Conclusion</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<p>Reiterates practical aim: striving to do better ethically is valuable even if perfection is unattainable. Emphasizes compassion, humor, and the social dimension of morality.</p>
</div>
</div>

</div>
</section>

<!-- ========================= Exercises ========================= -->
<section id="exercises" class="accordion-section">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-1"><span>Exercises</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">

<div class="accordion-subsection">
<button class="accordion-toggle level-2"><span>Practice Prompts</span></button>
<div class="accordion-content">
<ol>
<li>Identify a recent minor moral dilemma you faced. Write down the choices, who is affected, and apply three heuristics from the book to decide what you would do next time.</li>
<li>Run a short thought experiment: choose a policy you support and imagine the best and worst consequences. How does that change your confidence in supporting it?</li>
<li>Role-play a negotiation where you must balance your duty to another person with a conflicting general principle. Reflect on what trade-offs you accept.</li>
<li>Keep a one-week "moral journal": note daily decisions you consider morally relevant, the reasoning you used, and what you'd change after reflection.</li>
<li>Create a personal rule-of-thumb (e.g., "If I can prevent harm with minimal cost, I should.") and test it against three past scenarios—does it recommend sensible actions?</li>
</ol>
</div>
